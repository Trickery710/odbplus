P0303

Cylinder 3 Misfire Detected

Powertrain Generic (SAE) Severity: High

System

Ignition System / Fuel System

Subsystem

Misfire Detection — Cylinder 3

Common Fix

Spark Plug / Ignition Coil (Cyl. 1)

Repair Cost

$50 - $500+

What Does P0303 Mean?

P0303 indicates that the powertrain control module (PCM) has detected a misfire specifically in cylinder number 3. Unlike P0300 (random/multiple cylinder misfire), P0303 pinpoints the fault to a single cylinder, which significantly narrows the diagnostic process.

The PCM detects misfires by monitoring crankshaft rotational speed via the crankshaft position sensor. Each cylinder's power stroke produces a small but measurable acceleration of the crankshaft. When cylinder 3 fails to fire, or fires at the wrong time, the PCM detects the absence of that acceleration pulse and logs the misfire event. If the misfire rate exceeds a threshold over a defined number of crankshaft revolutions, P0303 is stored.

Important: A flashing check engine light means misfires are occurring at a rate severe enough to damage the catalytic converter. If the light is flashing, reduce speed and load immediately and have the vehicle repaired as soon as possible.

How Misfire Detection Works

Modern engines use crankshaft-based misfire detection rather than direct cylinder monitoring:

Components Involved in Cylinder 3 Firing:

  • Ignition Coil (Cylinder 3): Generates the high-voltage spark for cylinder 3. On coil-on-plug (COP) systems, each cylinder has its own coil directly above the plug.
  • Spark Plug (Cylinder 3): Delivers the spark into the combustion chamber to ignite the air/fuel mixture.
  • Fuel Injector (Cylinder 3): Sprays the precise quantity of fuel into cylinder 3's intake port or directly into the cylinder.
  • Crankshaft Position Sensor: Monitors crankshaft speed variations — the primary misfire detection input to the PCM.
  • PCM: Counts misfire events per cylinder per 200 and 1,000 crankshaft revolutions to determine if misfire thresholds are exceeded.

Common Symptoms

  • Rough or Shaking Idle

    Engine shakes noticeably with one cylinder not contributing power

  • Flashing or Steady Check Engine Light

    Flashing light indicates severe, catalyst-damaging misfire rate

  • Loss of Power and Acceleration

    One cylinder not firing reduces total engine output proportionally

  • Hesitation Under Load

    Stumbling or hesitation especially when accelerating from a stop

  • Increased Fuel Consumption

    Unburned fuel and compensatory fueling waste gas

  • Fuel or Burning Smell from Exhaust

    Unburned fuel or oil from the misfiring cylinder passes into the exhaust

Possible Causes

Because P0303 is specific to cylinder 3, start diagnosis with the components that are unique to that cylinder:

Ignition System — Cylinder 3

  • Worn or fouled spark plug (cylinder 3)
  • Failed ignition coil (cylinder 3)
  • Damaged spark plug wire (if equipped)
  • Cracked spark plug boot/coil boot
  • Carbon tracking on coil or plug

Fuel System — Cylinder 3

  • Clogged or stuck-closed fuel injector
  • Leaking fuel injector (flooded cylinder)
  • Low fuel pressure affecting cylinder 3
  • Injector wiring or connector fault

Mechanical — Cylinder 3

  • Low compression (worn rings or valves)
  • Burned or bent valve
  • Head gasket failure at cylinder 3
  • Valve timing issue (jumped chain/belt)
  • Excessive carbon on intake valve (direct injection)

Other Causes

  • Vacuum leak near cylinder 3 intake
  • Crankshaft position sensor fault
  • PCM driver circuit failure
  • Engine coolant or oil intrusion into cylinder

Diagnostic Steps

1

Confirm the Code and Locate Cylinder 3

Use your ODB+ scanner to confirm P0303 and check for companion codes. Identify the physical location of cylinder 3 on your engine — on most inline engines it is the frontmost cylinder; on V-engines, refer to the owner's manual as the numbering varies by manufacturer.

2

Swap the Coil to Another Cylinder

Move the cylinder 3 ignition coil to cylinder 2 (or any adjacent cylinder) and clear codes. If the misfire follows the coil to the new cylinder (e.g., P0302 appears), the coil is faulty. If P0303 remains, the coil is not the cause.

3

Inspect and Test the Spark Plug

Remove the cylinder 3 spark plug. Look for heavy carbon fouling (rich condition), oil fouling (worn seals or rings), white deposits (coolant leak), or a worn electrode gap. Replace if any of these conditions exist or if the plug is past its service interval.

4

Test the Fuel Injector

Use a mechanic's stethoscope or OBD+ injector balance test to confirm cylinder 3's injector is firing with the correct pulse. You can also swap the injector to another cylinder (if the design allows) to see if the misfire follows it. Check injector resistance with a multimeter (typically 10–18 ohms for high-impedance injectors).

5

Perform a Compression Test on Cylinder 3

If ignition and fuel components test fine, perform a compression test on cylinder 3. Compression should be within 10% of the other cylinders. Low compression indicates worn rings, a burned valve, or a head gasket leak. Follow up with a cylinder leak-down test for more detail.

6

Inspect for Vacuum Leaks Near Cylinder 3

A vacuum leak near cylinder 3's intake port causes that cylinder to run lean and misfire. Use carburetor cleaner or a smoke machine to find leaks in intake manifold gaskets, vacuum hoses, or throttle body gaskets specific to that cylinder's intake tract.

Common Repairs

Replace Spark Plug — Cylinder 3

Most common repair. Use the manufacturer-specified plug type and gap. Consider replacing all plugs if mileage warrants it.

DIY: $5-$25 Shop: $50-$150

Replace Ignition Coil — Cylinder 3

Second most common fix. Confirmed by the swap test described above.

DIY: $20-$80 Shop: $100-$250

Replace Fuel Injector — Cylinder 3

Required if injector balance test or swap confirms a clogged or faulty injector.

DIY: $50-$150 Shop: $150-$400

Engine Mechanical Repairs

Low compression repairs range from a valve job to a full engine rebuild depending on severity. Head gasket replacement is a significant repair.

DIY: $200-$600 Shop: $1,000-$4,000+
