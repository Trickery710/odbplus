P0171

System Too Lean (Bank 1)

Powertrain Generic (SAE) Severity: Medium

System

Fuel and Air Metering

Subsystem

Fuel Trim / O2 Sensor

Common Fix

Vacuum Leak / MAF Sensor

Repair Cost

$50 - $400

What Does P0171 Mean?

The P0171 code indicates that the engine control module (ECM) has detected that the air/fuel mixture on Bank 1 is running too lean. "Lean" means there's too much air and not enough fuel in the combustion mixture.

The ECM continuously monitors the oxygen (O2) sensors to determine if the fuel mixture is correct. When it detects a lean condition, it adds more fuel through the fuel injectors. The amount of extra fuel needed is tracked as "fuel trim." When long-term fuel trim exceeds approximately +25%, the P0171 code is set.

Bank 1 Location: On V6/V8 engines, Bank 1 is the side containing cylinder #1. On inline 4-cylinder engines, there's only one bank. Check your vehicle's repair manual to confirm which side is Bank 1.

How the Fuel System Works

Modern engines use closed-loop fuel control to maintain the ideal air/fuel ratio of 14.7:1 (stoichiometric). This ratio provides complete combustion and optimal catalytic converter operation.

Fuel Control Components:

  • Mass Airflow Sensor (MAF): Measures the amount of air entering the engine
  • Upstream O2 Sensor: Monitors exhaust oxygen content before the catalytic converter
  • Fuel Injectors: Spray precise amounts of fuel based on ECM calculations
  • Fuel Pressure Regulator: Maintains consistent fuel pressure to injectors
  • ECM (Engine Computer): Calculates fuel delivery based on sensor inputs

Understanding Fuel Trim

Fuel trim values show how much the ECM is adjusting fuel delivery:

  • 0%: No correction needed - running at calculated value
  • Positive values (+): ECM is adding fuel (lean condition)
  • Negative values (-): ECM is removing fuel (rich condition)
  • Normal range: -10% to +10%

Common Symptoms

  • Check Engine Light

    Usually steady, not flashing unless misfires occur

  • Rough or High Idle

    Engine may idle roughly or higher than normal as ECM compensates

  • Hesitation on Acceleration

    Stumbling or hesitation when pressing the gas pedal

  • Hard Starting

    Engine may be difficult to start, especially when cold

  • Decreased Fuel Economy

    Counterintuitively, a lean condition often hurts fuel economy due to incomplete combustion

  • Hissing Sound from Engine

    Audible vacuum leak, often more noticeable at idle

Possible Causes

A lean condition can be caused by either too much air entering the engine or not enough fuel being delivered:

Too Much Air (Most Common)

  • Vacuum leak (intake manifold gasket)
  • Cracked or disconnected vacuum hose
  • Leaking PCV valve or hose
  • Brake booster vacuum leak
  • Intake air duct leak after MAF sensor
  • Leaking EVAP purge valve

Sensor Issues

  • Dirty or faulty MAF sensor
  • Faulty upstream O2 sensor
  • Dirty or faulty MAP sensor
  • Faulty coolant temperature sensor

Fuel Delivery Problems

  • Weak fuel pump (low pressure)
  • Clogged fuel filter
  • Clogged or dirty fuel injectors
  • Leaking fuel pressure regulator

Other Causes

  • Exhaust leak before O2 sensor
  • Low engine compression
  • EGR system malfunction
  • PCM software needs update

Diagnostic Steps

1

Check Fuel Trim Values

Use your ODB+ scanner to view Short Term Fuel Trim (STFT) and Long Term Fuel Trim (LTFT) at idle and at 2500 RPM. Values over +10% confirm a lean condition. Note if the lean condition is worse at idle (suggests vacuum leak) or at higher RPM (suggests fuel delivery issue).

2

Listen and Look for Vacuum Leaks

With the engine running, listen for hissing sounds around the intake manifold, throttle body, and vacuum hoses. Visually inspect all rubber hoses for cracks or disconnections. Pay special attention to:

  • PCV valve and hose
  • Brake booster hose
  • Intake manifold gasket area
  • Throttle body gasket
3

Smoke Test (Recommended)

A smoke machine is the most effective way to find vacuum leaks. Smoke is introduced into the intake system and leaks become visible. Many auto parts stores rent smoke machines, or a shop can perform this test.

4

Inspect and Clean MAF Sensor

A contaminated MAF sensor is a very common cause of P0171. Remove the MAF sensor and inspect the sensing element for dirt or oil contamination. Clean with MAF sensor cleaner spray only - never touch the element or use other cleaners.

5

Check Fuel Pressure

If vacuum leaks and MAF sensor aren't the issue, test fuel pressure. Connect a fuel pressure gauge and compare to specifications (typically 40-60 PSI). Check pressure at idle, under acceleration, and watch for pressure drop over time (leaky injector).

6

Check for Exhaust Leaks

An exhaust leak before the O2 sensor can draw in outside air, making the sensor read lean. Inspect exhaust manifold gaskets and connections. Look for black soot marks indicating leaks.

Common Repairs

Clean MAF Sensor

Use MAF sensor cleaner to remove contamination from sensing element.

DIY: $8-$15 Shop: $50-$100

Replace Vacuum Hoses

Replace cracked or deteriorated vacuum lines causing leaks.

DIY: $10-$30 Shop: $75-$150

Replace Intake Manifold Gasket

Common leak point, especially on higher mileage vehicles.

DIY: $30-$80 Shop: $200-$500

Replace MAF Sensor

If cleaning doesn't help, MAF sensor may need replacement.

DIY: $80-$200 Shop: $150-$350

Replace PCV Valve

Stuck open PCV valve causes vacuum leak and lean condition.

DIY: $10-$30 Shop: $50-$100

Clean Fuel Injectors

Professional cleaning service or fuel injector cleaner additive.

DIY: $15-$30 Shop: $100-$200

Tech Tips

Toyota/Lexus

Check the air intake hose between the MAF and throttle body for cracks. These are notorious for developing splits on the underside that aren't visible from above.

Ford

The PCV elbow on many Ford engines cracks and causes P0171. Check the PCV system thoroughly before replacing other parts.

GM/Chevrolet

Intake manifold gaskets on 3.1L and 3.4L V6 engines commonly fail and cause lean codes. Check for coolant leaks as well.

Honda

Check the IACV (Idle Air Control Valve) gasket and the fast idle valve. These are common vacuum leak points on Honda engines.
