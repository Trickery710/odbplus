P0300

Random/Multiple Cylinder Misfire Detected

Powertrain Generic (SAE) Severity: High

System

Ignition System / Fuel System

Subsystem

Misfire Detection

Common Fix

Spark Plugs / Ignition Coils

Repair Cost

$50 - $500+

What Does P0300 Mean?

The P0300 code indicates that the vehicle's powertrain control module (PCM) has detected misfires occurring in multiple cylinders or randomly across all cylinders. Unlike codes P0301-P0308, which indicate misfires in specific cylinders, P0300 means the misfire pattern isn't isolated to a single cylinder.

A misfire occurs when the air/fuel mixture in a cylinder fails to ignite properly, or ignites at the wrong time. The PCM monitors crankshaft speed and detects the subtle changes in acceleration that occur when a cylinder doesn't produce its normal power.

Important: Driving with an active misfire can damage your catalytic converter. Unburned fuel entering the exhaust can overheat the catalyst, causing permanent and expensive damage. Address this code promptly.

How the Ignition System Works

For an engine to run smoothly, each cylinder must complete four strokes in perfect sequence: intake, compression, power, and exhaust. During the power stroke, the spark plug ignites the compressed air/fuel mixture at precisely the right moment.

Components Involved in Ignition:

  • Spark Plugs: Create the electrical spark that ignites the fuel mixture
  • Ignition Coils: Transform battery voltage (12V) into the high voltage (20,000-40,000V) needed to create a spark
  • Crankshaft Position Sensor: Tells the PCM exactly where each piston is in its cycle
  • Camshaft Position Sensor: Helps determine which cylinder is on its compression stroke
  • Fuel Injectors: Spray precise amounts of fuel into each cylinder
  • PCM (Engine Computer): Coordinates timing of spark and fuel delivery

Common Symptoms

  • Rough or Shaking Idle

    Engine vibrates noticeably at idle, may feel like it's about to stall

  • Hesitation or Stumbling

    Engine stumbles or hesitates during acceleration, especially under load

  • Loss of Power

    Noticeable reduction in acceleration and overall engine performance

  • Flashing Check Engine Light

    A flashing (not steady) light indicates active, severe misfiring - stop driving

  • Increased Fuel Consumption

    Unburned fuel and inefficient combustion lead to poor fuel economy

  • Fuel Smell from Exhaust

    Raw, unburned fuel odor from tailpipe indicates incomplete combustion

Possible Causes

Because P0300 indicates random misfires rather than a single cylinder issue, the cause is usually something that affects all cylinders:

Ignition System (Most Common)

  • Worn or fouled spark plugs
  • Failing ignition coils
  • Damaged spark plug wires (if equipped)
  • Distributor cap/rotor wear (older vehicles)
  • Ignition timing issues

Fuel System

  • Low fuel pressure (weak fuel pump)
  • Clogged fuel filter
  • Dirty or failing fuel injectors
  • Contaminated fuel (water in gas)
  • Fuel pressure regulator failure

Air/Vacuum System

  • Vacuum leaks (intake manifold gasket)
  • Mass airflow sensor (MAF) contamination
  • Dirty or clogged air filter
  • PCV valve issues
  • EGR system malfunction

Mechanical Issues

  • Low compression (worn rings/valves)
  • Jumped timing belt/chain
  • Head gasket leak
  • Valve adjustment issues
  • Carbon buildup on valves

Diagnostic Steps

1

Check for Related Codes

Use your ODB+ scanner to read all stored codes. Look for cylinder-specific misfire codes (P0301-P0308), fuel system codes (P0171, P0172), or sensor codes that might indicate the root cause. Note any freeze frame data.

2

Visual Inspection

Check for obvious issues: loose or damaged spark plug wires, cracked vacuum hoses, disconnected sensors, or visible damage to ignition components. Listen for vacuum leaks (hissing sound) with the engine running.

3

Inspect Spark Plugs

Remove and examine the spark plugs. Look for:

  • Heavy carbon deposits: Indicates rich running condition
  • Oil fouling: Possible worn rings or valve seals
  • White/blistered: Engine running too hot or lean
  • Worn electrode: Plugs due for replacement
4

Check Fuel Pressure

Connect a fuel pressure gauge to test fuel system pressure at idle and under load. Compare readings to manufacturer specifications. Low pressure can cause lean misfires across all cylinders.

5

Monitor Live Data

Use ODB+ live data to monitor:

  • Long-term fuel trim: Values over +10% indicate lean condition
  • O2 sensor readings: Should oscillate between rich and lean
  • MAF sensor: Compare to expected values at idle
  • Engine coolant temp: Should reach operating temperature
6

Compression Test (If Needed)

If ignition and fuel systems check out, perform a compression test. All cylinders should be within 10% of each other. Low compression in multiple cylinders suggests internal engine wear or timing issues.

Common Repairs

Replace Spark Plugs

Most common fix. Use OEM or high-quality plugs gapped to specification.

DIY: $20-$80 Shop: $100-$250

Replace Ignition Coils

Coil-on-plug systems may need one or all coils replaced.

DIY: $50-$200 Shop: $150-$400

Repair Vacuum Leak

Replace cracked hoses or intake manifold gasket.

DIY: $10-$50 Shop: $100-$400

Replace Fuel Injectors

Clean or replace clogged/failing fuel injectors.

DIY: $150-$400 Shop: $400-$900

Replace Fuel Pump

If fuel pressure is low, pump replacement may be needed.

DIY: $150-$300 Shop: $400-$800
