P0507

Idle Air Control System RPM Higher Than Expected

Fuel/Air Metering Generic (SAE) Severity: Medium

System

Idle Speed Control

Subsystem

Air Metering

Common Fix

Find/Fix Vacuum Leak

Repair Cost

$50 - $400

What Does P0507 Mean?

The P0507 code indicates that the engine idle speed is higher than the PCM (powertrain control module) expects. The PCM monitors idle speed and attempts to maintain it within a specific range (typically 600-900 RPM for most vehicles). When the actual idle exceeds the target by more than a calibrated amount (often 200+ RPM) despite the PCM's attempts to lower it, this code is set.

In other words, something is causing the engine to get more air than it should at idle, and the PCM can't compensate enough to bring the RPM back down to normal.

Vacuum Leaks: The most common cause of P0507 is a vacuum leak that allows unmetered air to enter the engine. The PCM doesn't know about this extra air, causing a lean condition that raises idle speed.

How the Idle Control System Works

Modern vehicles use different methods to control idle speed, but the principle is the same - regulate the amount of air bypassing the throttle plate:

Idle Control Methods:

  • Electronic Throttle Body (Drive-by-Wire): PCM controls the throttle plate position directly via an electric motor. Most common on 2005+ vehicles.
  • Idle Air Control (IAC) Valve: A stepper motor or solenoid that opens/closes an air passage around the throttle plate. Common on older vehicles.
  • Throttle Body Idle Adjustment: Some systems adjust the throttle plate's minimum position using a motor or adjustment screw.

How It Works: The PCM continuously monitors engine RPM and adjusts idle air flow to maintain target speed. If RPM drops (from A/C compressor engaging, for example), the PCM increases air flow. If RPM is too high, it reduces air flow. P0507 sets when RPM stays high despite the PCM minimizing idle air.

Common Symptoms

  • High Idle Speed

    Engine idles noticeably faster than normal (often 1000-1500+ RPM)

  • Check Engine Light

    P0507 code stored, may also see lean codes (P0171, P0174)

  • Hissing or Whistling Sound

    Audible vacuum leak noise from under the hood

  • Rough or Surging Idle

    Engine RPM may fluctuate up and down as PCM tries to compensate

  • Vehicle Creeps Forward in Gear

    High idle can cause automatic transmission to push vehicle forward when stopped

  • Hard Engagement When Shifting

    Shifting from Park/Neutral into gear may feel harsh due to high RPM

Possible Causes

Vacuum Leaks (Most Common)

  • Cracked or disconnected vacuum hoses
  • Leaking intake manifold gasket
  • Brake booster vacuum leak
  • PCV valve or hose leak
  • Cracked intake manifold
  • Throttle body gasket leak

Throttle Body Issues

  • Carbon buildup on throttle plate
  • Throttle body needs cleaning
  • Throttle body motor malfunction
  • Sticking throttle plate
  • Incorrect throttle body relearn

IAC Valve Problems

  • IAC valve stuck open
  • IAC valve carbon buildup
  • IAC electrical failure
  • IAC passage blocked or leaking

Other Causes

  • Faulty TPS (throttle position sensor)
  • EGR valve stuck open
  • PCM needs reprogramming
  • Incorrect idle air adjustment
  • Alternator overcharging

Diagnostic Steps

1

Check for Vacuum Leaks

With engine running, listen for hissing sounds. Spray carburetor cleaner around vacuum hoses, intake manifold gasket, and throttle body gasket - if idle changes when sprayed, you've found a leak. Alternatively, use a smoke machine for easier detection.

2

Monitor Scanner Data

Use your ODB+ scanner to check actual idle RPM vs. desired idle. Also check:

  • Long-term fuel trim (positive values indicate lean/vacuum leak)
  • Throttle position at idle (should be near 0%)
  • IAC duty cycle or counts (if equipped)
  • MAF sensor readings
3

Inspect Throttle Body

Remove the air intake hose and visually inspect the throttle body. Look for carbon buildup around the throttle plate edges and idle air passages. If dirty, cleaning may solve the problem.

4

Test IAC Valve (If Equipped)

On vehicles with a separate IAC valve, unplug it while the engine is running. If idle drops significantly and the valve is commanding closed but allowing air, the IAC is stuck open or bypassed.

5

Perform Throttle Body Relearn

After repairs or throttle body cleaning, many vehicles require an idle relearn procedure. This can often be done with a scanner or by following a specific sequence (consult service manual).

Common Repairs

Repair Vacuum Leak

Replace cracked hoses, gaskets, or PCV valve causing the leak.

DIY: $10-$50 Shop: $75-$250

Clean Throttle Body

Remove carbon buildup from throttle plate and bore using throttle body cleaner.

DIY: $5-$15 Shop: $75-$150

Replace IAC Valve

Replace stuck or malfunctioning idle air control valve.

DIY: $40-$100 Shop: $150-$300

Replace Intake Manifold Gasket

Seal vacuum leak at intake manifold to cylinder head joint.

DIY: $30-$100 Shop: $200-$500

Perform Idle Relearn Procedure

Reset throttle body adaptive values after repairs or cleaning.

DIY: $0 Shop: $50-$100
