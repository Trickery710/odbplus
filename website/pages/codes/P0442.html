P0442

Evaporative Emission System Leak Detected (Small Leak)

Powertrain Generic (SAE) Severity: Low

System

EVAP / Emissions

Subsystem

Fuel Vapor Recovery

Common Fix

Gas Cap / EVAP Hose

Repair Cost

$20 - $400

What Does P0442 Mean?

The P0442 code indicates that the vehicle's EVAP (Evaporative Emission Control) system has detected a small leak. The EVAP system captures fuel vapors from the gas tank and stores them in a charcoal canister, then purges them into the engine to be burned rather than releasing them into the atmosphere.

A "small leak" is typically defined as a leak equivalent to a 0.020" to 0.040" diameter hole. The system uses either a vacuum test or pressure test to detect these leaks, depending on the vehicle manufacturer.

Start Simple: The #1 cause of P0442 is a loose, damaged, or missing gas cap. Before doing extensive diagnostics, try tightening or replacing the gas cap and driving for a few days to see if the code clears.

How the EVAP System Works

The EVAP system prevents fuel vapors from escaping into the atmosphere. Here's how it works:

EVAP System Components:

  • Fuel Tank: Source of fuel vapors, sealed except through EVAP system
  • Gas Cap: Seals the filler neck - a common leak point
  • EVAP Canister: Contains activated charcoal that absorbs fuel vapors
  • Purge Valve: Opens to allow vapors to be drawn into the engine
  • Vent Valve: Controls air flow into the system for purging
  • EVAP Lines: Hoses connecting components
  • Fuel Tank Pressure Sensor: Monitors system pressure/vacuum

Leak Detection

The ECM tests the EVAP system for leaks under specific conditions (usually engine off, certain temperature range). It seals the system and monitors pressure changes. If pressure/vacuum changes faster than expected, a leak is detected. The size of the leak determines whether P0442 (small), P0455 (large), or P0456 (very small) is set.

Common Symptoms

  • Check Engine Light

    Usually steady light, may take several drive cycles to appear

  • Failed Emissions Test

    Vehicle will fail emissions inspection with this code

  • Fuel Smell (Sometimes)

    You may notice a faint fuel odor, especially near the rear of the vehicle

  • No Driveability Issues

    P0442 typically doesn't affect how the vehicle drives

Possible Causes

Gas Cap Issues (Most Common)

  • Loose gas cap
  • Damaged or worn cap seal
  • Wrong cap for vehicle
  • Cracked cap

EVAP Hoses & Connections

  • Cracked or dry-rotted hoses
  • Loose hose connections
  • Damaged quick-connect fittings
  • Hose rubbed through

EVAP Components

  • Leaking purge valve
  • Cracked EVAP canister
  • Faulty vent valve
  • Leaking fuel tank pressure sensor

Fuel Tank & Filler

  • Cracked filler neck
  • Rust holes in fuel tank
  • Damaged tank seal/gasket
  • Fuel pump seal leak

Diagnostic Steps

1

Check the Gas Cap

Remove the gas cap and inspect the rubber seal for cracks, deterioration, or debris. Make sure the cap clicks when tightened. If the seal looks worn or damaged, replace the cap. Clear the code and drive to see if it returns.

2

Visual Inspection

Inspect all visible EVAP hoses and connections. Look for cracks, loose connections, or damage. Pay attention to hoses near heat sources or areas where they might rub against other components. Check the charcoal canister (usually near the fuel tank) for cracks.

3

Smoke Test

A smoke machine is the most effective way to find EVAP leaks. Smoke is introduced into the system through the EVAP service port or gas tank filler. Watch for smoke escaping from the leak point. This test can find even very small leaks.

4

Check Purge and Vent Valves

Use a scan tool to command the purge and vent valves. Listen for clicking when they activate. A valve that's stuck open can cause leak codes. You can also apply vacuum to test if valves hold properly.

5

Check Fuel Tank Area

If the leak isn't found above the vehicle, inspect underneath. Look at the fuel tank seams, the filler neck connection, and the fuel pump access seal (if applicable). Rust and corrosion can cause leaks in older vehicles.

Common Repairs

Replace Gas Cap

Most common and cheapest fix. Use OEM or quality aftermarket cap.

DIY: $10-$30 Shop: $30-$50

Replace EVAP Hoses

Replace cracked or damaged vapor hoses.

DIY: $10-$40 Shop: $75-$150

Replace Purge Valve

Replace faulty EVAP purge solenoid.

DIY: $30-$80 Shop: $100-$200

Replace Vent Valve

Replace faulty EVAP canister vent valve.

DIY: $40-$100 Shop: $100-$250

Replace EVAP Canister

Replace cracked or saturated charcoal canister.

DIY: $80-$200 Shop: $200-$400
