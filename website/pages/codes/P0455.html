P0455

Evaporative Emission System Leak Detected (Large Leak)

Emissions Generic (SAE) Severity: Low-Medium

System

EVAP Emissions Control

Subsystem

Leak Detection

Common Fix

Gas Cap / EVAP Hoses

Repair Cost

$0 - $300

What Does P0455 Mean?

The P0455 code indicates that the vehicle's powertrain control module (PCM) has detected a large leak in the Evaporative Emission Control (EVAP) system. The EVAP system is designed to capture fuel vapors from the gas tank and store them in a charcoal canister, preventing them from escaping into the atmosphere.

A "large leak" typically means a leak equivalent to a 0.080" diameter hole or larger - often caused by something as simple as a loose or missing gas cap. The PCM performs periodic leak tests and triggers this code when the system can't maintain the expected vacuum level.

Good News: P0455 is often caused by a loose gas cap - the cheapest and easiest fix possible. Before spending money on diagnostics, check your gas cap first. Tighten it until it clicks, clear the code, and see if it returns.

How the EVAP System Works

The EVAP system is an emissions control system that prevents gasoline vapors from escaping into the atmosphere. Here's how it operates:

EVAP System Components:

  • Fuel Tank: Source of fuel vapors that must be contained
  • Gas Cap: Seals the fuel tank - a critical component
  • Charcoal Canister: Stores fuel vapors using activated charcoal
  • Purge Valve: Opens to allow stored vapors to enter the engine for burning
  • Vent Valve: Controls airflow into the canister; closes during leak tests
  • EVAP Lines: Hoses connecting all components
  • Fuel Tank Pressure Sensor: Monitors system pressure during tests

Common Symptoms

  • Check Engine Light

    Usually the only symptom - vehicle typically runs normally

  • Fuel Smell Near Vehicle

    Noticeable gasoline odor, especially near the fuel tank or filler neck

  • Failed Emissions Test

    Vehicle will not pass inspection with this code present

  • Slight Decrease in Fuel Economy

    Fuel vapors escaping instead of being burned can marginally affect MPG

Possible Causes

A large EVAP leak is usually caused by a significant opening in the system:

Gas Cap Issues (Most Common)

  • Gas cap not tightened (no click)
  • Missing gas cap
  • Damaged gas cap seal
  • Wrong gas cap for vehicle
  • Cracked gas cap housing

EVAP Hose Problems

  • Disconnected EVAP hose
  • Cracked or split hose
  • Hose knocked loose by road debris
  • Rodent damage to hoses
  • Deteriorated rubber connections

Valve Failures

  • Purge valve stuck open
  • Vent valve stuck open
  • Failed valve solenoid
  • Cracked valve housing

Other Components

  • Cracked charcoal canister
  • Damaged fuel tank
  • Failed filler neck
  • Fuel tank pressure sensor issue
  • Leaking fuel sending unit O-ring

Diagnostic Steps

1

Check the Gas Cap

Remove the gas cap and inspect the seal. Look for cracks, tears, or debris on the sealing surface. Reinstall and tighten until you hear it click (usually 2-3 clicks). Clear the code and drive for a few days to see if it returns.

2

Visual Inspection

Inspect all visible EVAP hoses under the hood and under the vehicle. Look for disconnected, cracked, or damaged hoses. Check the charcoal canister (usually near rear axle) for damage. Look for obvious signs of rodent activity.

3

Smoke Test

The most effective diagnostic method for EVAP leaks is a smoke test. Special equipment introduces smoke into the EVAP system, and leaks become visible as smoke escapes. Most shops charge $50-$100 for this test.

4

Check Purge and Vent Valves

Use your ODB+ scanner to command the purge valve closed during the test (if supported). If the system still won't hold vacuum, the leak is external. If it holds vacuum when the purge valve is closed, suspect the purge valve itself.

5

Inspect Under the Vehicle

Check the fuel tank, filler neck, and EVAP canister for physical damage. Road debris can crack components. Look at connections where hoses attach - these are common leak points.

Common Repairs

Tighten or Replace Gas Cap

First thing to try - often solves the problem immediately.

DIY: $0-$25 Shop: $25-$50

Replace EVAP Hose

Replace cracked or disconnected hoses with proper fuel-rated hose.

DIY: $10-$50 Shop: $75-$150

Replace Purge Valve

Electronic solenoid valve that controls vapor flow to engine.

DIY: $30-$80 Shop: $100-$250

Replace Vent Valve

Controls airflow into EVAP canister; often located near canister.

DIY: $30-$100 Shop: $100-$300

Replace Charcoal Canister

Replace if cracked or saturated with liquid fuel.

DIY: $100-$200 Shop: $200-$400
