Intake Air Temperature (IAT) Sensor

Also known as: Air Charge Temperature (ACT), Manifold Air Temperature (MAT)

Temperature Fuel Calculation Moderate

Location

Air Intake or MAF Housing

Signal Type

Variable Resistance (NTC)

Lifespan

150,000+ miles

Replacement Cost

$15 - $80

What Is an IAT Sensor?

The Intake Air Temperature (IAT) sensor measures the temperature of the air entering the engine. This information is vital for the Engine Control Module (ECM) to calculate air density and adjust fuel delivery accordingly - cold air is denser and requires more fuel than hot air for the same air mass reading.

The IAT sensor is commonly located in the air intake tube, integrated into the MAF sensor housing, or in the intake manifold. On turbocharged vehicles, an additional IAT sensor may be located after the intercooler to measure charge air temperature.

Speed-Density Systems: On vehicles without a MAF sensor (speed-density systems), the IAT sensor is especially critical. The ECM calculates air mass using MAP, IAT, and RPM data. A faulty IAT can significantly impact fuel mixture accuracy.

How IAT Sensors Work

Thermistor Technology

The IAT sensor uses a thermistor - a resistor that changes resistance based on temperature. Most IAT sensors use a Negative Temperature Coefficient (NTC) thermistor, meaning resistance decreases as temperature increases. This is the same technology used in coolant temperature sensors.

Signal Circuit

The ECM supplies a 5-volt reference voltage to the sensor. As the thermistor resistance changes with temperature, it creates a voltage divider effect. The ECM reads this varying voltage (typically 0.5V to 4.5V) and converts it to a temperature value using a calibration table.

Air Density Correction

The IAT reading allows the ECM to correct for air density changes. Hot air is less dense (fewer oxygen molecules per volume) than cold air. Without IAT compensation, hot weather would cause a rich condition, and cold weather would cause lean operation.

Normal IAT Sensor Readings

Temperature Typical Resistance Typical Voltage
-4°F (-20°C) 13,000 - 17,000 ohms 4.0 - 4.5V
32°F (0°C) 5,000 - 7,000 ohms 3.3 - 3.8V
68°F (20°C) 2,000 - 3,000 ohms 2.5 - 3.0V
104°F (40°C) 1,000 - 1,500 ohms 1.5 - 2.0V
176°F (80°C) 250 - 400 ohms 0.5 - 1.0V

Cold Start Check: On a cold engine (sitting overnight), the IAT reading should closely match the ambient temperature and the ECT reading. Large differences indicate a sensor problem.

Symptoms of a Failing IAT Sensor

  • Check Engine Light with P0110-P0114 Codes

    IAT circuit malfunction, range/performance, or signal codes

  • Hard Starting in Cold Weather

    Sensor reading too warm causes lean cold-start mixture

  • Rough Idle or Stalling

    Incorrect air density calculation affects idle fuel mixture

  • Decreased Fuel Economy

    Incorrect fuel mixture wastes fuel

  • Hesitation During Acceleration

    Fuel mixture doesn't match actual air conditions

  • Unrealistic Temperature Readings

    Scanner shows -40°F or 300°F regardless of actual temperature

What Causes IAT Sensor Problems?

Sensor Failure

  • Thermistor degradation
  • Internal open circuit
  • Internal short circuit
  • Physical damage

Contamination

  • Oil from aftermarket air filters
  • Dirt/dust buildup
  • PCV oil mist
  • Carbon deposits

Wiring Issues

  • Damaged wiring harness
  • Corroded connector pins
  • Poor ground connection
  • Water intrusion

Installation Issues

  • Sensor not fully seated
  • Heat soak from engine
  • Incorrect sensor location
  • Loose connection

How to Test an IAT Sensor

1

Cold Engine Comparison

On a cold engine, compare IAT reading to ECT (coolant temperature) using your ODB+ scanner. Both should be within a few degrees of ambient temperature. Large differences indicate a problem with one of the sensors.

2

Check for Realistic Values

Monitor IAT while driving. It should increase slightly above ambient due to engine heat but remain reasonable (typically 10-40°F above ambient). Readings stuck at -40°F indicate an open circuit; stuck at 300°F indicates a short.

3

Resistance Test

Disconnect the sensor and measure resistance across the terminals. Compare to temperature/resistance specifications. Test at different temperatures by warming the sensor with a heat gun (carefully) to verify it changes correctly.

4

Check Reference Voltage

With sensor disconnected and ignition on, check for 5V reference on the supply wire. If missing, check wiring back to the ECM or look for a shared 5V reference circuit problem.

5

Visual Inspection

Inspect the sensor tip for contamination, damage, or corrosion. Check the connector for bent pins, corrosion, or water damage. Clean any contamination carefully.
