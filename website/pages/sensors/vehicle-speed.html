Vehicle Speed Sensor (VSS)

Also known as: Speed Sensor, Output Shaft Speed Sensor, Speedometer Sensor

Transmission ABS/Stability Important

Location

Transmission/Transfer Case/Wheel Hub

Signal Type

Hall Effect or Magnetic AC

Lifespan

100,000+ miles

Replacement Cost

$30 - $150

What Is a Vehicle Speed Sensor?

The Vehicle Speed Sensor (VSS) measures how fast the vehicle is moving and sends this information to multiple control modules. This data is critical for the speedometer display, transmission shift points, cruise control, ABS operation, and many other vehicle systems.

Modern vehicles typically have multiple speed sensors - a transmission output shaft sensor, individual wheel speed sensors for ABS, and sometimes an input shaft sensor. The term "VSS" traditionally refers to the transmission-mounted sensor that provides overall vehicle speed data.

VSS vs. Wheel Speed Sensors: The VSS measures drivetrain speed at the transmission, while wheel speed sensors (WSS) measure individual wheel rotation for ABS/traction control. Many modern vehicles calculate vehicle speed from wheel sensors rather than a dedicated VSS.

How Vehicle Speed Sensors Work

Hall Effect Sensors

Most modern VSS use Hall effect technology. A toothed reluctor ring attached to the transmission output shaft passes by the sensor. The sensor produces a digital square wave signal with frequency proportional to speed. These sensors require power and ground to operate.

Magnetic (Variable Reluctance) Sensors

Older vehicles use magnetic sensors that generate their own AC voltage as the reluctor passes. The signal amplitude and frequency both increase with speed. These are passive sensors requiring no external power but have weaker output at low speeds.

Signal Processing

The ECM or TCM counts the pulses from the VSS to calculate vehicle speed. A typical sensor produces 4,000-8,000 pulses per mile, depending on the gear tooth count and final drive ratio. The system calibrates this to convert pulses to actual speed.

Normal VSS Readings

VSS output varies by sensor type and vehicle design:

Condition Hall Effect Output Magnetic Output
Stopped 0 Hz (steady state) No signal
25 MPH ~300-500 Hz 0.5-1.5V AC @ same Hz
60 MPH ~700-1200 Hz 2-5V AC @ same Hz

Scanner Reading: When monitoring VSS via ODB+, you should see 0 MPH when stopped and speed that closely matches your actual speedometer reading while driving.

Symptoms of a Failing VSS

  • Speedometer Not Working

    Speedometer reads 0 or displays erratic readings while driving

  • Transmission Shift Problems

    Hard shifts, no upshifts, or stuck in one gear (limp mode)

  • Cruise Control Inoperative

    Cruise control won't engage or maintain speed

  • Check Engine Light with P0500-P0503

    VSS circuit malfunction, range/performance, or intermittent codes

  • ABS/Traction Control Light

    Safety systems may deactivate due to missing speed data

  • Torque Converter Clutch Issues

    TCC may not engage or engage at wrong times

What Causes VSS Problems?

Sensor Failure

  • Internal circuit failure
  • Heat damage
  • Physical damage
  • Age-related wear

Mechanical Issues

  • Damaged drive gear
  • Worn reluctor teeth
  • Incorrect air gap
  • Gear oil contamination

Wiring Problems

  • Damaged wiring harness
  • Corroded connector
  • Poor ground connection
  • Broken wires from vibration

Contamination

  • Metal debris on sensor tip
  • Transmission fluid leak
  • Road salt/dirt intrusion
  • Water damage

How to Test a Vehicle Speed Sensor

1

Check Scanner Speed Data

Use your ODB+ scanner to monitor vehicle speed. With wheels off the ground (safely supported), rotate the drive wheels by hand. You should see speed increase on the scanner. No reading indicates a sensor or circuit problem.

2

Compare to Speedometer

While driving, compare scanner speed to speedometer. If they don't match, the issue may be in the speedometer itself. If both read zero, the VSS circuit is likely at fault.

3

Visual Inspection

Inspect the sensor, connector, and wiring for damage. Check for metal debris on the sensor tip (magnetic sensors attract metal). Look for gear oil leaks around the sensor mounting area.

4

Check Power and Ground (Hall Effect)

With ignition on, verify 5V or 12V (depending on type) on the supply wire and good ground. If power is missing, check fuses and wiring back to the module.

5

Test Signal Output

Using a multimeter or oscilloscope, check for signal output while rotating the drive wheels. Hall effect sensors should produce a square wave; magnetic sensors should produce AC voltage.
