EVAP Pressure Sensor

Also known as: Fuel Tank Pressure Sensor, FTP Sensor, EVAP System Pressure Sensor

Emissions Control EVAP System Important

Location

Fuel Tank or EVAP Canister

Signal Type

0-5V Analog

Lifespan

150,000+ miles

Replacement Cost

$50 - $200

What Is an EVAP Pressure Sensor?

The EVAP pressure sensor (also called fuel tank pressure sensor or FTP sensor) monitors pressure and vacuum in the evaporative emissions system. This is essential for the EVAP leak detection test that ensures fuel vapors are properly contained and not released into the atmosphere.

The EVAP system captures fuel vapors from the tank and stores them in a charcoal canister until the engine can burn them. The pressure sensor allows the ECM to detect leaks as small as 0.020 inches (about the size of a pinhole) by monitoring how the system holds pressure or vacuum during testing.

Common Trigger: The most common cause of EVAP codes is a loose or missing gas cap. Before diagnosing EVAP pressure sensor issues, always check that the gas cap is properly tightened and the seal is in good condition.

How EVAP Pressure Sensors Work

Pressure Measurement

The sensor uses a strain gauge or piezoelectric element to measure pressure relative to atmosphere. It's designed to measure small pressures - typically -1.5 to +1.5 PSI (or approximately -10 to +10 inches of water column). This sensitivity is necessary to detect tiny leaks.

EVAP Leak Test Process

  1. ECM waits for proper conditions (engine off, cool temperatures, specific fuel level)
  2. System seals the EVAP system by closing the purge and vent valves
  3. ECM applies slight vacuum or monitors natural vacuum from fuel cooling
  4. Pressure sensor monitors how well the system holds vacuum over time
  5. Excessive pressure loss indicates a leak - code is set

Normal Operations

During normal driving, fuel tank pressure varies with temperature and fuel usage. The sensor monitors this continuously. When the tank builds slight pressure (on hot days or during refueling), the ECM opens the purge valve to relieve pressure through the engine rather than venting to atmosphere.

Normal EVAP Pressure Sensor Readings

Condition Pressure Reading Typical Voltage
Atmospheric (gas cap off) 0 in. H2O 2.4 - 2.6V
Normal Tank (sealed) -1 to +2 in. H2O 2.3 - 2.8V
Hot Day (pressure) +2 to +4 in. H2O 2.7 - 3.2V
During Purge -5 to -15 in. H2O 1.5 - 2.2V
Leak Test Vacuum -7 to -10 in. H2O 1.8 - 2.0V

Key Point: Voltage around 2.5V indicates atmospheric pressure (no pressure or vacuum). Lower voltage = vacuum, higher voltage = pressure. With gas cap removed, reading should be at or near atmospheric.

Symptoms of a Failing EVAP Pressure Sensor

  • Check Engine Light with P0440-P0457 Codes

    EVAP system malfunction, leak detected, or sensor codes

  • P0452/P0453 - FTP Sensor Low/High

    Sensor circuit reading out of expected range

  • Difficulty Refueling

    Fuel pump nozzle keeps clicking off due to improper venting

  • Fuel Smell

    Though often caused by actual leaks, sensor failure can mask leak detection

  • Failed Emissions Test

    EVAP system readiness monitor won't complete

  • Hissing Sound When Opening Gas Cap

    Indicates vacuum buildup from improper venting (may or may not be sensor-related)

What Causes EVAP Pressure Sensor Problems?

Sensor Failure

  • Internal element degradation
  • Circuit board failure
  • Contamination from fuel vapors
  • Physical damage

System Issues

  • Loose or damaged gas cap
  • Cracked EVAP hoses
  • Failed purge valve
  • Failed vent valve

Connection Problems

  • Corroded connector
  • Damaged wiring
  • Poor ground connection
  • Water intrusion

Tank Issues

  • Tank damage/rust
  • Filler neck leak
  • Sending unit seal
  • Tank deformation
Test Conditions Matter: EVAP leak tests only run under specific conditions - typically after the vehicle has been sitting for several hours with the engine cold. If you clear codes and immediately try to complete the EVAP monitor, it won't run. Normal driving over several days may be needed.

How to Test an EVAP Pressure Sensor

1

Check Gas Cap First

Verify the gas cap is tight and the seal is in good condition. This is the most common cause of EVAP codes. Some vehicles have a dedicated gas cap warning.

2

Monitor Live Data

Use your ODB+ scanner to view fuel tank pressure with the gas cap on and off. With cap off, reading should go to atmospheric (~0 in. H2O or ~2.5V). If it doesn't change, the sensor or circuit has a problem.

3

Compare to Atmospheric

With gas cap removed, FTP sensor voltage should read approximately 2.5V (mid-scale). Readings stuck high or low indicate sensor failure or wiring issues.

4

Check During Purge

Monitor FTP reading while commanding the purge valve open (if scanner supports bidirectional controls). You should see vacuum increase (voltage drop) as engine vacuum pulls on the tank.

5

Smoke Test for Leaks

If sensor tests good but leak codes persist, use an EVAP smoke machine to find actual leaks in hoses, connections, or the tank. The sensor may be accurately detecting a real leak.
