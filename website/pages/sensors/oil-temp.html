Oil Temperature Sensor

Also known as: Engine Oil Temperature (EOT) Sensor, Oil Temp Sender

Temperature Protection Moderate

Location

Oil Pan, Block, or Oil Filter Housing

Signal Type

Variable Resistance (NTC)

Lifespan

Engine lifetime

Replacement Cost

$20 - $100

What Is an Oil Temperature Sensor?

The oil temperature sensor monitors the temperature of the engine oil. This information is used by the Engine Control Module (ECM) to optimize engine performance, determine oil life, and protect the engine from damage due to overheating or operating with cold, thick oil.

While coolant temperature is the primary indicator of engine warmth, oil temperature provides additional insights. Oil takes longer to warm up than coolant and can become dangerously hot under extreme conditions even when coolant temperature appears normal.

Oil Life Monitoring: Many modern vehicles use the oil temperature sensor as part of their oil life monitoring system. The ECM tracks how often and how hot the oil gets to calculate when an oil change is needed, rather than relying solely on mileage.

How Oil Temperature Sensors Work

Thermistor Design

Like the coolant temperature and intake air temperature sensors, the oil temperature sensor uses a thermistor element. Most use a Negative Temperature Coefficient (NTC) thermistor that decreases resistance as temperature rises. The sensor tip is immersed in the oil to measure its temperature directly.

ECM Functions Using Oil Temperature

  • Cold start enrichment: Extended warm-up fuel enrichment until oil reaches operating temperature
  • Rev limiter adjustment: Lower rev limit during cold operation to protect bearings
  • Variable valve timing: Adjust VVT operation based on oil viscosity (affected by temperature)
  • Oil life calculation: Track thermal stress on oil for maintenance reminders
  • Overtemperature protection: Reduce power or warn driver if oil overheats

Normal Oil Temperature Readings

Condition Temperature Range Notes
Cold Start Ambient (32-75°F / 0-24°C) Should match air/coolant temp
Warm-up Phase 100-180°F (38-82°C) Gradually increasing
Normal Operation 195-230°F (90-110°C) Optimal viscosity range
Heavy Load/Hot Day 230-260°F (110-127°C) Still acceptable
Dangerous >280°F (>138°C) Oil breakdown risk

Key Point: Oil temperature typically runs 20-50°F higher than coolant temperature under normal conditions. Oil takes longer to warm up than coolant during cold starts.

Symptoms of a Failing Oil Temperature Sensor

  • Check Engine Light with P0195-P0199 Codes

    Oil temperature circuit malfunction or range codes

  • Inaccurate Oil Temperature Gauge

    Gauge reads too high, too low, or doesn't move

  • Incorrect Oil Life Readings

    Oil life monitor resets early or doesn't change

  • Extended Warm-up Behavior

    ECM thinks oil is cold, keeps engine in warm-up mode too long

  • Reduced Engine Protection

    ECM can't detect overheating oil condition

What Causes Oil Temperature Sensor Problems?

Sensor Failure

  • Internal thermistor failure
  • Contamination from oil breakdown
  • Heat damage over time
  • Physical damage during service

Connection Issues

  • Corroded connector pins
  • Oil contamination in connector
  • Damaged wiring harness
  • Poor ground connection

Oil-Related Issues

  • Low oil level exposing sensor
  • Degraded/contaminated oil
  • Wrong oil viscosity
  • Sludge buildup on sensor

Installation Problems

  • Cross-threaded sensor
  • Damaged sealing washer
  • Over/under torqued
  • Wrong sensor for application

How to Test an Oil Temperature Sensor

1

Cold Engine Check

On a cold engine (sitting overnight), compare oil temperature to coolant temperature and ambient temperature using your ODB+ scanner. All three should be within a few degrees of each other.

2

Monitor During Warm-up

Watch oil temperature as the engine warms up. It should gradually increase, eventually settling 20-50°F above coolant temperature. Readings that don't change or change erratically indicate a problem.

3

Visual Inspection

Inspect the sensor and connector for oil contamination, corrosion, or damage. Check that the sensor is properly installed and not leaking. Verify the oil level is adequate.

4

Resistance Test

Remove the sensor and measure resistance at known temperatures. Compare to specifications - typically 150-200 ohms at 212°F (100°C). An open or shorted reading indicates failure.

5

Check Circuit Voltage

With sensor disconnected and ignition on, check for 5V reference. If present, reconnect and check signal voltage - should vary with temperature. Fixed high or low voltage indicates a wiring issue.
