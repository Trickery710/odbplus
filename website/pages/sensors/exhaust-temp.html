Exhaust Gas Temperature (EGT) Sensor

Also known as: Exhaust Temperature Sensor, EGTS, Pre-Cat/Post-Cat Temp Sensor

Temperature DPF/Catalyst Critical (Diesel)

Location

Exhaust Manifold/DPF/Turbo

Signal Type

Variable Resistance (PTC/NTC)

Lifespan

80,000 - 120,000 miles

Replacement Cost

$80 - $250

What Is an EGT Sensor?

The Exhaust Gas Temperature (EGT) sensor measures the temperature of exhaust gases at various points in the exhaust system. This data is critical for managing emissions control systems, particularly the Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF) on diesel engines and catalytic converters on gas engines.

Modern diesel vehicles typically have multiple EGT sensors - before and after the turbocharger, and before and after the DPF. Gasoline vehicles with turbos or advanced emissions systems may also have EGT sensors to protect components and optimize catalyst operation.

DPF Regeneration: On diesel vehicles, EGT sensors are essential for DPF regeneration. The ECM monitors exhaust temperature to determine when the DPF needs cleaning and whether conditions are suitable for regeneration (exhaust temps typically need to reach 1000-1100°F / 540-600°C).

How EGT Sensors Work

Sensor Types

NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient): Used for lower temperature ranges (up to ~1000°F). Resistance decreases as temperature increases.

PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient): Used for higher temperature ranges. Resistance increases with temperature. Common in extreme-temperature locations like turbo outlets.

Thermocouple: Found on some performance applications. Generates a voltage based on temperature difference, can measure very high temperatures.

Typical EGT Sensor Locations

  • EGT 1: Before turbocharger (monitors exhaust manifold temps)
  • EGT 2: After turbocharger (monitors turbo outlet)
  • EGT 3: Before DPF/catalyst (determines if regen is possible)
  • EGT 4: After DPF/catalyst (confirms regen effectiveness)

Normal EGT Sensor Readings

Condition Pre-Turbo Post-DPF
Idle (Warm) 300-450°F (150-230°C) 250-400°F (120-200°C)
Light Load 500-700°F (260-370°C) 400-600°F (200-315°C)
Heavy Load 900-1200°F (480-650°C) 700-1000°F (370-540°C)
DPF Regeneration 1000-1300°F (540-700°C) 1000-1200°F (540-650°C)

Temperature Drop: EGT should decrease as exhaust flows through the system (turbo extracts heat, DPF absorbs heat). If post-DPF temp is higher than pre-DPF, regeneration may be occurring.

Symptoms of a Failing EGT Sensor

  • Check Engine Light with P0544-P0549 or P2031-P2034

    EGT sensor circuit codes indicating malfunction

  • DPF Warning Light (Diesel)

    System cannot verify regeneration temperature is reached

  • Frequent DPF Regeneration Attempts

    ECM keeps initiating regen due to incorrect temperature data

  • Reduced Engine Power / Limp Mode

    ECM limits power to protect exhaust components

  • Poor Fuel Economy (Diesel)

    Incomplete regeneration cycles waste fuel

  • Turbo Protection Issues

    ECM can't protect turbo from overheating without accurate EGT data

What Causes EGT Sensor Problems?

Extreme Heat Exposure

  • Repeated high-temperature cycles
  • DPF regeneration heat stress
  • Turbo overboost conditions
  • Material fatigue from thermal cycling

Contamination

  • Soot buildup on sensor tip
  • Oil contamination from turbo
  • Coolant contamination (head gasket)
  • Carbon deposits

Physical Damage

  • Cracked sensor from thermal shock
  • Vibration damage
  • Impact damage during service
  • Corrosion from exhaust chemicals

Wiring Issues

  • Heat-damaged wiring insulation
  • Corroded connector from heat cycling
  • Broken wires from vibration
  • Water intrusion in connector
Burn Hazard: EGT sensors and surrounding components can reach temperatures over 1000°F during operation and remain hot long after shutdown. Never touch exhaust components without verifying they have cooled. Allow several hours for complete cooling.

How to Test an EGT Sensor

1

Compare Multiple EGT Readings

Using your ODB+ scanner, compare readings from all EGT sensors. At idle, all sensors should read within a reasonable range of each other (accounting for position). One sensor reading drastically different suggests a problem with that specific sensor.

2

Cold Start Comparison

On a cold engine, all EGT sensors should read approximately ambient temperature. A sensor reading very high or very low on a cold engine has failed.

3

Monitor During Warm-up

Watch EGT values as the engine warms up and under varying loads. All sensors should respond consistently to changes. Erratic or non-responsive readings indicate a failing sensor.

4

Visual Inspection (Engine Cold)

Inspect the sensor, wiring, and connector for damage, corrosion, or heat damage. Check that the sensor is properly installed and the connector is fully seated.

5

Resistance Test (Engine Cold)

With the sensor at room temperature, measure resistance and compare to specifications. NTC sensors typically read 500-1500 ohms at room temp. Open or shorted readings indicate failure.
