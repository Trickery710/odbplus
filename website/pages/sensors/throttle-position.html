Throttle Position Sensor (TPS)

Also known as: Accelerator Position Sensor, Throttle Angle Sensor

Engine Management Fuel Control Critical

Location

Throttle Body

Signal Type

0.5V - 4.5V (Position)

Lifespan

100,000+ miles

Replacement Cost

$50 - $200

What Is a Throttle Position Sensor?

The Throttle Position Sensor (TPS) monitors the position of the throttle plate (butterfly valve) in the throttle body. It tells the Engine Control Module (ECM) how far you're pressing the accelerator pedal, allowing the ECM to adjust fuel injection, ignition timing, and transmission shift points accordingly.

Modern vehicles with electronic throttle control (drive-by-wire) have multiple position sensors - typically two on the accelerator pedal (APP sensors) and two in the throttle body (TPS). This redundancy allows the ECM to verify readings and detect faults.

Electronic Throttle: On modern vehicles with electronic throttle control, the TPS doesn't directly connect to the accelerator pedal. Instead, APP sensors read pedal position, and the ECM commands the throttle motor. The TPS confirms the throttle position matches what was commanded.

How the TPS Works

Potentiometer Type (Traditional)

Traditional TPS uses a potentiometer - a variable resistor with a wiper that moves along a resistive track as the throttle opens. The ECM supplies a 5V reference voltage, and the TPS returns a signal voltage proportional to throttle angle:

  • Closed throttle (idle): ~0.5V
  • Part throttle (cruising): ~1.5V - 3.0V
  • Wide open throttle (WOT): ~4.5V

Hall Effect Type (Modern)

Modern TPS often uses Hall effect sensors instead of potentiometers. These are non-contact sensors that measure the position of a magnet attached to the throttle shaft. Hall effect sensors are more durable because there's no physical contact to wear out.

What the ECM Does with TPS Data:

  • Fuel delivery: Calculates appropriate fuel pulse width based on throttle angle and engine speed
  • Acceleration enrichment: Adds extra fuel during rapid throttle opening
  • Deceleration fuel cut: Cuts fuel during closed-throttle deceleration
  • Transmission shifts: Adjusts shift points based on throttle position
  • Traction control: Monitors driver demand for traction management

Normal TPS Readings

Throttle Position Typical Voltage Typical Percentage
Closed (Idle) 0.4V - 0.8V 0% - 5%
Light Cruise 1.0V - 2.0V 15% - 25%
Moderate Accel 2.0V - 3.5V 30% - 60%
Wide Open Throttle 4.0V - 4.8V 95% - 100%

Important: The voltage should change smoothly and continuously as you slowly press the accelerator. Any "dead spots" or sudden voltage jumps indicate a worn sensor.

Symptoms of a Failing TPS

  • Check Engine Light

    TPS codes (P0120-P0124) indicate circuit or performance problems

  • Hesitation or Stumbling

    Engine hesitates or stumbles when accelerating, especially from a stop

  • Surging or Bucking

    Vehicle surges forward or bucks at steady throttle due to erratic signal

  • Rough or Unstable Idle

    Idle speed fluctuates or engine runs rough at idle

  • Lack of Power

    Vehicle feels sluggish or won't accelerate properly

  • Transmission Shifting Issues

    Erratic or harsh shifts due to incorrect throttle position data

  • Limp Mode

    Vehicle enters reduced power mode (especially with electronic throttle)

How to Test a TPS

1

Monitor Live Data

Use your ODB+ scanner to view TPS voltage or percentage in real-time. With the engine off and key on, slowly press the accelerator from closed to wide open throttle. The reading should increase smoothly without any sudden jumps or dropouts.

2

Check for Dead Spots

A worn potentiometer-type TPS often has "dead spots" where the wiper doesn't make good contact. Graph the TPS voltage while slowly moving the throttle - look for any sudden spikes or flat spots in the graph.

3

Voltage Test with Multimeter

Backprobe the TPS signal wire and measure voltage to ground while slowly opening the throttle. Voltage should increase smoothly from ~0.5V to ~4.5V. Tap on the sensor while monitoring - if voltage jumps, the sensor has internal wear.

4

Check Reference Voltage

Verify the ECM is supplying 5V reference to the TPS. With the sensor unplugged, measure voltage between the 5V reference and ground wires in the connector. Should read very close to 5.0V.

5

Inspect Wiring and Connector

Check for corroded pins, damaged wires, or loose connections. The TPS connector is exposed to engine heat and can deteriorate over time.

Replacement Tips

Check for Adjustment

Some older TPS units are adjustable - loosen the screws and rotate the sensor to set the correct idle voltage. Most modern sensors are non-adjustable and must be positioned correctly during installation.

Use OEM or Quality Parts

Cheap aftermarket TPS units often have poor accuracy or fail quickly. For electronic throttle systems, always use OEM or OEM-equivalent quality sensors.

Relearn Procedure

Many vehicles require a throttle body or TPS relearn procedure after replacement. This can often be done with a scan tool, or by following a specific key-on sequence in the owner's manual.

Clean Throttle Body

While the TPS is removed, clean the throttle body and throttle plate. Carbon buildup can affect idle quality and throttle response.
