Engine Coolant Temperature (ECT) Sensor

Also known as: Coolant Temp Sensor, CTS, Water Temperature Sensor

Engine Management Fuel Control Critical

Location

Cylinder Head or Thermostat Housing

Signal Type

Variable Resistance (NTC)

Lifespan

100,000+ miles

Replacement Cost

$15 - $100

What Is an ECT Sensor?

The Engine Coolant Temperature (ECT) sensor measures the temperature of the engine coolant. This information is critical for the Engine Control Module (ECM) to adjust fuel injection, ignition timing, and other engine parameters based on whether the engine is cold, warming up, or at operating temperature.

The ECT sensor is typically a thermistor - a resistor whose resistance changes with temperature. Most vehicles use a Negative Temperature Coefficient (NTC) thermistor, meaning resistance decreases as temperature increases.

Two Sensors: Many vehicles have two coolant temperature sensors - one for the ECM (engine management) and one for the temperature gauge on the dashboard. They may be in different locations and have different specifications.

How the ECT Sensor Works

The ECT sensor is a two-wire sensor. The ECM sends a reference voltage (typically 5V) through one wire. The sensor's internal thermistor provides a path to ground through the second wire. As coolant temperature changes, the thermistor's resistance changes, which changes the voltage the ECM reads.

Typical Resistance Values (NTC Thermistor):

Temperature Resistance (Approx.) Voltage (Approx.)
-4°F (-20°C) 15,000 - 25,000 ohms 4.0 - 4.5V
68°F (20°C) 2,000 - 3,000 ohms 3.0 - 3.5V
176°F (80°C) 300 - 400 ohms 1.0 - 1.5V
212°F (100°C) 150 - 200 ohms 0.5 - 0.8V

What the ECM Does with ECT Data:

  • Cold engine: Enriches fuel mixture, advances timing, increases idle speed
  • Warm-up: Gradually leans out mixture, adjusts timing
  • Operating temp: Uses closed-loop fuel control with O2 sensor feedback
  • Overheating: May retard timing, reduce power, activate cooling fans

Symptoms of a Failing ECT Sensor

  • Check Engine Light

    ECT sensor codes (P0115-P0119) indicate circuit or performance issues

  • Hard Starting When Cold

    Engine may be difficult to start when cold if sensor reports incorrect temperature

  • Poor Fuel Economy

    If sensor reads colder than actual, ECM will over-fuel the engine

  • Rough Idle When Cold

    Engine may run rough during warm-up period

  • Temperature Gauge Issues

    Gauge reads incorrectly or erratically (if using same sensor)

  • Cooling Fan Issues

    Fans may run constantly or not turn on when needed

  • Black Smoke from Exhaust

    Rich running condition due to ECM thinking engine is cold

How to Test an ECT Sensor

1

Check Live Data

Use your ODB+ scanner to read ECT temperature with the engine cold. It should match ambient temperature (within a few degrees). Then warm up the engine and verify the temperature rises smoothly to operating temp (typically 180-220°F / 82-104°C).

2

Compare to IAT

On a cold engine that has sat overnight, compare ECT to Intake Air Temperature (IAT). They should be within a few degrees of each other. A large difference indicates a problem with one sensor.

3

Resistance Test

With the sensor unplugged and engine cold, measure resistance across the sensor terminals with a multimeter. Compare to specifications for your vehicle. Test again with engine warm - resistance should be much lower.

4

Voltage Test

With sensor connected, backprobe the signal wire and measure voltage to ground. Cold engine should read 3-4V, warm engine should read 0.5-1.5V. Verify smooth change as engine warms up.

5

Check Wiring

Inspect the connector for corrosion, damaged pins, or loose connections. Check wiring for chafing or damage. Test continuity from ECM connector to sensor connector.

Common Causes of Failure

Sensor Failure

  • Internal thermistor degradation
  • Open or shorted circuit
  • Corrosion on terminals
  • Physical damage

Wiring Issues

  • Corroded connector
  • Damaged wires
  • Poor ground connection
  • Water intrusion

Coolant System

  • Low coolant level exposing sensor
  • Air pocket around sensor
  • Contaminated coolant

Other Issues

  • Stuck thermostat affecting actual temp
  • ECM reference voltage issue
  • Using wrong replacement sensor

Replacement Tips

Drain Coolant First

Drain coolant below the sensor level before removal to prevent spills. Some sensors can be swapped quickly with minimal coolant loss, but have rags ready.

Use Thread Sealant

Apply Teflon tape or thread sealant to the sensor threads to prevent leaks. Don't get sealant on the sensor tip.

Torque Properly

Don't overtighten - ECT sensors are typically torqued to 10-15 ft-lbs. Overtightening can crack the housing or damage the sensor.

Bleed Air After

After refilling coolant, bleed air from the system. Air pockets can cause erratic readings and overheating.
