Camshaft Position (CMP) Sensor

Also known as: Cam Sensor, CMP, Camshaft Synchronizer

Engine Management Timing Critical

Location

Cylinder Head (Near Camshaft)

Signal Type

Hall Effect or Magnetic

Lifespan

100,000 - 150,000 miles

Replacement Cost

$50 - $200

What Is a Camshaft Position Sensor?

The Camshaft Position (CMP) sensor monitors the position and rotational speed of the camshaft. This information is critical for the Engine Control Module (ECM) to synchronize fuel injection timing on sequential fuel injection systems and to determine which cylinder is in its compression stroke.

The CMP sensor works in conjunction with the Crankshaft Position (CKP) sensor. While the CKP tells the ECM the exact position of the crankshaft, the CMP identifies which stroke each cylinder is in - compression or exhaust - since each cylinder goes through both during a complete engine cycle.

VVT Engines: On engines with Variable Valve Timing (VVT), the camshaft position sensor is essential for monitoring the actual camshaft position versus commanded position. Multiple CMP sensors may be present - one for intake cam and one for exhaust cam on DOHC engines.

How Camshaft Position Sensors Work

Hall Effect Sensors (Most Common)

Hall effect CMP sensors use a semiconductor chip that responds to changes in magnetic field. A reluctor wheel or target attached to the camshaft passes by the sensor, creating changes in the magnetic field. The sensor outputs a clean digital square wave signal - either high (5V) or low (0V) - making it easy for the ECM to read precise timing.

Magnetic (Variable Reluctance) Sensors

These sensors generate their own AC voltage signal as the reluctor wheel passes. The signal amplitude and frequency increase with camshaft speed. Found on older vehicles and some heavy-duty applications. They don't require external power but produce weaker signals at low RPM.

Reluctor Wheel Patterns

The pattern on the reluctor wheel varies by manufacturer. Some use a single notch or tooth for cylinder #1 identification, while others use multiple teeth for more precise position data. On V-engines, each bank typically has its own CMP sensor.

Normal CMP Sensor Readings

CMP sensor output depends on the sensor type and engine design:

Sensor Type Signal Output Notes
Hall Effect 0V / 5V Square Wave Clean on/off signal at any RPM
Magnetic (idle) 0.3 - 1.0V AC Low amplitude at slow speeds
Magnetic (2500 RPM) 2.0 - 5.0V AC Amplitude increases with RPM

Key Relationship: The CMP sensor signal frequency should be exactly half of the CKP frequency (since the camshaft rotates at half crankshaft speed). Any deviation may indicate timing chain/belt issues.

Symptoms of a Failing CMP Sensor

  • Check Engine Light with P0340-P0349 Codes

    CMP circuit malfunction, no signal, or correlation codes

  • No Start or Hard Starting

    Engine cranks but won't start, or takes longer than normal to fire

  • Engine Stalling

    Engine dies intermittently while driving or at idle

  • Rough Running or Misfires

    Incorrect fuel injection timing causes poor combustion

  • Poor Fuel Economy

    ECM may revert to batch fuel injection without proper CMP signal

  • Hesitation During Acceleration

    Engine stumbles or hesitates during acceleration

What Causes CMP Sensor Problems?

Sensor Failure

  • Heat damage from engine bay temps
  • Internal circuit failure
  • Cracked housing from vibration
  • Age and wear

Wiring Issues

  • Corroded connector pins
  • Damaged wiring harness
  • Poor ground connection
  • Water intrusion

Mechanical Issues

  • Damaged reluctor wheel
  • Incorrect sensor air gap
  • Timing chain stretch
  • Jumped timing belt

Oil Contamination

  • Oil leaks from cam seal
  • Contaminated connector
  • Oil on reluctor wheel
CMP vs. Timing Issues: Before replacing a CMP sensor for correlation codes (P0016-P0019), check the timing chain or belt. A stretched timing chain will cause the same symptoms and codes as a bad CMP sensor but won't be fixed by sensor replacement.

How to Test a CMP Sensor

1

Check for Codes

Use your ODB+ scanner to read codes. CMP codes include P0340-P0349 (circuit codes) and P0016-P0019 (CKP/CMP correlation codes). Correlation codes may indicate timing issues rather than sensor failure.

2

Visual Inspection

Inspect the sensor, connector, and wiring for damage, corrosion, or oil contamination. Check that the connector is fully seated and locked.

3

Check Power and Ground (Hall Effect)

With ignition on, check for 5V reference on the supply wire and good ground. If either is missing, trace the circuit back to the ECM.

4

Check Signal During Cranking

Using a multimeter or scope, verify the sensor produces a signal while cranking. Hall effect sensors should pulse between 0V and 5V. Magnetic sensors should produce an AC voltage.

5

Compare CKP and CMP Timing

Using a scope or advanced scanner, compare CKP and CMP signals. Any deviation from the expected relationship may indicate timing chain/belt issues.
