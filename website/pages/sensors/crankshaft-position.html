Crankshaft Position Sensor (CKP)

Also known as: Crank Sensor, CPS, Engine Speed Sensor

Engine Management Critical

Location

Near Crankshaft Pulley or Flywheel

Signal Type

AC Voltage or Digital Square Wave

Lifespan

100,000+ miles

Replacement Cost

$50 - $250

What Is a Crankshaft Position Sensor?

The Crankshaft Position Sensor (CKP) is one of the most critical sensors in the engine management system. It monitors the rotational speed and position of the crankshaft, providing the Engine Control Module (ECM) with essential data for ignition timing, fuel injection timing, and engine speed (RPM) calculation.

Without a working CKP sensor, the engine cannot run - the ECM has no way to know when to fire the spark plugs or inject fuel. This is why a failing CKP sensor often causes sudden stalling or no-start conditions.

Critical Sensor: A completely failed CKP sensor will prevent the engine from starting. If your car stalls suddenly and won't restart, but the starter cranks normally, the CKP sensor is a prime suspect.

How the CKP Sensor Works

The CKP sensor reads a toothed wheel (reluctor ring) attached to the crankshaft. As the teeth pass by the sensor, they create a signal that the ECM uses to determine crankshaft position and speed.

Magnetic (Variable Reluctance) Type

Uses a permanent magnet wrapped with a coil of wire. As each tooth passes, it creates a change in the magnetic field that induces an AC voltage in the coil. The frequency of this signal corresponds to engine RPM, and the amplitude increases with speed. These sensors typically have two wires.

Hall Effect Type

Uses a semiconductor that responds to changes in magnetic field. Produces a clean digital square wave signal regardless of engine speed. These sensors require a power supply and typically have three wires (power, ground, signal). More common on modern vehicles.

The Reluctor Ring

The toothed wheel typically has a missing tooth (or teeth) that creates a signature in the signal. This "missing tooth" position corresponds to a specific crankshaft position (usually near TDC for cylinder 1), allowing the ECM to determine exact crankshaft position, not just speed.

What the ECM Does with CKP Data:

  • Ignition timing: Determines exactly when to fire each spark plug
  • Fuel injection: Times fuel injection for optimal combustion
  • RPM calculation: Provides engine speed for gauge and other systems
  • Misfire detection: Detects slight variations in crankshaft acceleration
  • Tachometer signal: Drives the RPM gauge on the dashboard

Symptoms of a Failing CKP Sensor

  • No Start / Crank No Start

    Engine cranks but won't start - most common symptom of complete failure

  • Intermittent Stalling

    Engine suddenly dies while driving, often restarts after cooling down

  • Check Engine Light

    CKP codes (P0335-P0339) stored in the ECM

  • Hard Starting

    Extended cranking before engine fires, especially when hot

  • Engine Misfires

    Rough running, misfires, or hesitation due to incorrect timing

  • Tachometer Not Working

    RPM gauge reads zero or erratically (on some vehicles)

  • Vibration or Rough Running

    Engine runs rough due to timing variations

How to Test a CKP Sensor

1

Check for RPM Signal

Connect your ODB+ scanner and crank the engine. If the RPM reading stays at 0 during cranking, the ECM isn't receiving a valid CKP signal. This is a quick way to confirm a CKP-related problem.

2

Visual Inspection

Inspect the sensor and wiring for obvious damage. Check the connector for corrosion, bent pins, or oil contamination. Examine the sensor tip for metal debris (can indicate reluctor wheel damage) or damage from contact with the reluctor.

3

Resistance Test (Magnetic Type)

With the sensor unplugged, measure resistance across the two terminals. Typical range is 200-2000 ohms (check your vehicle's specs). Open circuit (infinite resistance) or very low resistance indicates a failed sensor.

4

Voltage Output Test (Magnetic Type)

Set your multimeter to AC volts. Connect to the sensor terminals and crank the engine. You should see 0.2V - 2V AC during cranking. No voltage means the sensor isn't generating a signal.

5

Signal Test (Hall Effect Type)

Use a digital multimeter or scope to check for a pulsing DC signal during cranking. The signal should switch between 0V and 5V (or close to reference voltage). A noid light can also be used to verify signal presence.

6

Check Air Gap

Some sensors are adjustable. The gap between sensor tip and reluctor wheel should typically be 0.5mm - 1.5mm. Too large a gap can cause weak or missing signals, especially at low RPM.

Common Causes of Failure

Heat Related

  • Internal component degradation from heat cycles
  • Cracked or melted plastic housing
  • Heat-induced intermittent failures

Physical Damage

  • Sensor tip contact with reluctor
  • Damaged or worn reluctor teeth
  • Impact damage during service

Electrical Issues

  • Corroded connector terminals
  • Damaged wiring harness
  • Poor ground connection
  • Oil contamination in connector

Installation Issues

  • Incorrect air gap
  • Improper torque causing cracking
  • Wrong sensor for application

Replacement Tips

Note Sensor Location

CKP sensors can be located near the crankshaft pulley (front of engine), near the flywheel (back of engine), or on the engine block. Identify your sensor location before ordering parts.

Check the Reluctor

While the sensor is out, inspect the reluctor wheel for damaged or missing teeth, debris, or excessive runout. A damaged reluctor can cause intermittent CKP problems.

Use Proper Torque

Don't overtighten the mounting bolt. CKP sensors are often plastic and can crack under excessive torque. Follow manufacturer specifications.

Check for Relearn

Some vehicles require a CKP relearn procedure after sensor replacement. This teaches the ECM the exact position of the reluctor wheel. Check service information for your specific vehicle.
