Knock Sensor

Also known as: Detonation Sensor, KS, Ping Sensor

Engine Management Protection Critical

Location

Engine Block or Cylinder Head

Signal Type

Piezoelectric AC Voltage

Lifespan

150,000+ miles

Replacement Cost

$50 - $300 (labor varies)

What Is a Knock Sensor?

The knock sensor is essentially a microphone that listens for abnormal combustion sounds in the engine, specifically engine knock (also called detonation, pinging, or pre-ignition). When knock is detected, the Engine Control Module (ECM) retards ignition timing to prevent engine damage.

Engine knock occurs when the air/fuel mixture ignites from pressure and heat before the spark plug fires, or when multiple flame fronts collide in the combustion chamber. This creates a characteristic metallic pinging sound and can quickly destroy pistons, rings, and bearings if left unchecked.

Performance Impact: A failed knock sensor forces the ECM to run conservative timing (retarded) to protect the engine. This results in reduced power and fuel economy. The engine runs safely but not optimally.

How Knock Sensors Work

Piezoelectric Principle

Knock sensors use piezoelectric crystals that generate a small voltage when subjected to vibration or pressure. The sensor is tuned to respond to the specific frequencies associated with engine knock - typically between 5,000 and 15,000 Hz. Normal engine vibration at other frequencies is filtered out.

Resonant vs. Flat Response Types

Resonant sensors are tuned to a narrow frequency band centered on the expected knock frequency for that engine. They're more sensitive to knock but may miss knock at different frequencies.

Flat response (broadband) sensors detect a wider range of frequencies and are more common on modern engines. The ECM uses digital signal processing to identify knock patterns within the broader signal.

ECM Processing

The ECM constantly monitors the knock sensor signal and compares it against expected noise levels for the current operating conditions. When knock is detected, the ECM immediately retards ignition timing (typically 2-3 degrees per knock event) until the knock stops, then gradually advances timing back to optimal.

Symptoms of a Failing Knock Sensor

  • Check Engine Light with P0325-P0334 Codes

    Knock sensor circuit malfunction or low/high input codes

  • Reduced Engine Performance

    Noticeable lack of power, especially during acceleration or under load

  • Decreased Fuel Economy

    Retarded timing from failed sensor reduces efficiency

  • Audible Knocking or Pinging

    If sensor fails to detect knock, engine may actually knock under load

  • Hesitation During Acceleration

    Engine may stumble or hesitate when accelerating

What Causes Knock Sensor Problems?

Sensor Issues

  • Internal piezoelectric element failure
  • Heat damage over time
  • Physical damage
  • Manufacturing defect

Installation Problems

  • Incorrect torque specification
  • Dirty mounting surface
  • Wrong sensor for application
  • Damaged threads

Wiring Issues

  • Damaged wiring harness
  • Corroded connector
  • Poor shielding (interference)
  • Water intrusion

Engine Conditions

  • Excessive carbon buildup
  • Low octane fuel usage
  • Overheating engine
  • EGR system problems
Torque is Critical: Knock sensors must be torqued to exact specifications - typically 15-20 lb-ft. Over-tightening or under-tightening changes the sensor's resonant frequency, causing false readings or reduced sensitivity. Always use a torque wrench.

How to Test a Knock Sensor

1

Read Diagnostic Codes

Use your ODB+ scanner to check for knock sensor codes. Also check for any codes that might indicate actual knock conditions (lean codes, timing codes) that could cause legitimate knock detection.

2

Check Knock Retard Data

Monitor "knock retard" or "timing advance" with your scanner. Under normal driving, knock retard should be 0 or minimal. Constant retard without audible knock suggests a sensor or wiring issue.

3

Tap Test

With the engine running and monitoring knock retard, carefully tap near the knock sensor with a wrench. A working sensor should register this as knock and show timing retard on your scanner.

4

Check Wiring and Connector

Inspect the sensor connector and wiring for damage, corrosion, or water intrusion. Check for proper shielding if equipped. Measure resistance according to manufacturer specs (typically 100K-500K ohms).

5

Verify Mounting Torque

If replacing or reinstalling, ensure proper torque. A loose sensor won't detect knock properly, while an over-tightened sensor may give false knock readings.
